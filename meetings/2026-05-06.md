# Meeting Assets for AI Agent Protocol CG Meeting

**Date**: 2026-05-06

## Quick Recap

The W3C AI Agent Protocol Community Group meeting on May 6, 2026 focused on a debrief from the W3C AC 2026 meeting in Hangzhou and on adjacent WebMCP work being discussed in W3C. Song shared a non-confidential summary of agentic-web topics from the AC meeting, including presentations from Google and Microsoft on WebMCP, its proposed declarative and imperative APIs, and its goal of exposing structured web-page capabilities to AI agents. ## Discussion Topics

### I. Meeting Logistics and AI Note-Takers

Before the main agenda, participants discussed how to handle AI note-takers or AI bots joining W3C calls. Song noted that W3C has been discussing AI-tool usage in meetings, but that policy remains difficult when an individual participant joins a meeting and also uses an AI tool to take personal notes.

Roy noted that he had declined an AI note-taking request for this meeting. Song asked Roy to continue rejecting incoming AI note-taker requests because some of the material being discussed came from the W3C AC meeting and should be shared only at an appropriate, non-confidential level.

The group recognized that there is a difference between rejecting an unrepresented AI bot and addressing a participant's own use of AI tooling. Song noted that this remains an open governance and policy issue for W3C meetings.

### II. W3C AC 2026 Debrief and Agentic-Web Context

Song gave a short, selected briefing from the **W3C AC 2026 meeting in Hangzhou**, emphasizing that the full AC meeting included sensitive material and would not be repeated in detail. Instead, he focused on agentic-web topics relevant to the AI Agent Protocol CG.

The AC discussion included broad strategic topics such as AI trends, AI regulation, organizational strategy, conflicts, formal objections, and other W3C-wide matters. For this CG, Song highlighted technical discussion around the fragmented state of agentic-web work and the gap between current web design and emerging AI-agent interaction models.

Song explained that Google and Microsoft presented WebMCP-related material and discussed the need for better structured interaction between AI agents and web content. He summarized WebMCP as an attempt to bridge ordinary web pages and AI agents by providing structured capabilities rather than relying only on scraping, screenshots, simulated clicks, and fragile UI automation.

### III. WebMCP Overview: Layers, APIs, and Roadmap

Song summarized the WebMCP framing presented at the AC meeting. The work was described as a possible stack for agentic web interaction, with layers covering:

- Baseline visibility and structured instruction extraction
- Site-declared capabilities through declarative APIs
- Imperative JavaScript APIs for registering custom tools and capabilities
- Trust and policy considerations, including possible relevance of W3C DID and Verifiable Credentials

The WebMCP roadmap discussed features such as:

- Registration and discovery
- Human-in-the-loop flows
- Cross-frame or cross-origin iframe support
- Declarative model-context elements
- Extension APIs

Song noted that WebMCP was presented as a way for websites to safely expose structured capabilities to AI agents. He also highlighted a slide claiming that WebMCP could reduce token consumption by giving agents more direct structured access to page capabilities rather than forcing them to infer intent from raw page content or screenshots.

### IV. Google and Microsoft WebMCP Presentation Themes

Song summarized points from the Google presentation. Google framed the current state of AI-agent interaction with websites as fragile because many agents scrape pages and simulate user clicks. Examples such as navigating a Gmail-like interface illustrated why purely visual or DOM-level automation can be slow, brittle, and unreliable.

The presentation contrasted this with the idea of an open web designed for agents, building on existing web mechanisms such as stateless and stateful sessions, cookies, local storage, URL discovery, authentication, and declarative UI. Song noted that security remains a major issue because a malicious or prompt-injected agent could steal data or take harmful actions.

Song also summarized points from the Microsoft presentation. Microsoft described WebMCP as enabling autonomous task completion on the web, where AI agents can understand, navigate, and act within web pages on behalf of users, while still supporting human confirmation when needed. Examples included agents searching for locations, retrieving details from websites, presenting recommendations, checking weather, booking appointments, shopping, or booking flights.

The group also discussed the relationship between raw DOM structures and accessibility trees. A license-renewal form example was used to show how labels, relationships, roles, form actions, contact information, and office hours can make pages understandable not only to human users and assistive technologies, but also to AI agents. Song described this semantic layer as an important foundation for WebMCP.

### V. WebMCP Evaluation Criteria, Incubation, and Open Questions

Song summarized five evaluation criteria discussed for WebMCP:

- **Performance**
- **Accuracy**
- **Capability**
- **Privacy**
- **Developer involvement**

The goal is for a standard to be fast, reliable, powerful, secure, and developer-friendly while allowing web developers to participate actively in building the agentic web.

Song noted that WebMCP has been incubated in the **W3C Web Machine Learning Community Group**, rather than as a direct web version of Anthropic MCP. The group discussed the view that WebMCP should be a web-native API effort, not merely an adaptation of Anthropic MCP. Contributors from Microsoft, Google, and other organizations were mentioned as participating in the effort.

Chris observed that there are many active participants on the WebML mailing list. Roy noted that Mozilla had objected to related AI API work, including prompt and translation APIs, which may help explain why Mozilla participation in this area appears limited. Song also observed that Apple/Safari participation was not evident in the CG discussion he had seen.

Open topics identified for WebMCP included:

- Security
- Privacy
- Accessibility
- Common tools
- Discovery
- Standardization path
- Relationship to browser sandboxes and server-side execution

### VI. Security, Browser Sandboxing, and Relationship to the Existing Web

Chris asked how WebMCP is fundamentally different from the existing web, especially once authentication, server-side computation, and private server-side material are involved. He compared the issue to the historical evolution from anonymous static web pages to interactive server-side systems such as CGI scripts, where the server may hold private keys or other privileged material.

Chris asked whether WebMCP's security model goes beyond the browser sandbox and whether the WebMCP ecosystem has addressed server-side security concerns, authentication, DID-related private key handling, and privileged execution contexts.

Song agreed that security and privacy were among the main questions raised at the AC meeting, especially when autonomous agents are delegated tasks on behalf of users. He said he did not yet have a complete answer from the WebMCP presentations and planned to look further into Chrome trial material and WebMCP security discussions.

Song also shared a comparison he had seen in the Chinese community between WebMCP and Anthropic MCP. In that comparison, Anthropic MCP uses JSON-oriented protocol mechanisms, while WebMCP is aiming at native web APIs. Song also noted that Anthropic MCP authentication work is associated with OAuth 2.1, while WebMCP may try to reuse existing web mechanisms such as sessions and cookies to support broad adoption by existing websites.

### VII. Related Workshops and Ecosystem Signals

Victor asked whether there had been feedback from the **AIW / Agentic Internet Workshop**. Sean indicated that he had joined AIW remotely. Victor explained that, at least from his U.S. ecosystem view, much of the current production discussion is centered around **MCP** and **A2A**, particularly through the Linux Foundation and Kubernetes/CNCF-related infrastructure where many agentic AI systems may run.

Victor noted that separate discussions are also happening in the OpenID Foundation and DIF around centralized versus decentralized identity protocols. He suggested that AIW and related agentic protocol meetings in California may be important venues for finding common ground between these protocol communities.

Song also mentioned attending part of a **GOSIM Paris** event, a general AI meetup sponsored by CSDN, Huawei, and others. He observed presentations from French companies and discussion of topics such as Mistral AI, OpenHarmony, and agentic design in operating systems.

### VIII. First-Year CG Deliverables and Publication Path

Song said that the AI Agent Protocol CG is approaching its first anniversary and proposed publishing more formal outputs from the group.

He identified two possible document tracks:

1. **Existing white paper and use cases**  
   Song suggested that the current white paper and use-case documents, already published in the CG GitHub repository and contributed to by universities, Ant Group, and other CG members, could be prepared as a CG final report or technical note and published through the W3C website.

2. **Protocol guidance document, starting with agent identification**  
   Song suggested producing a protocol document that initially focuses on **agent identification**. The document could later be extended to cover authentication, discovery, and other mechanisms. He noted that existing identification material has been referenced by the AMP community and that feedback so far suggests the minimum design set is useful and stable enough to consider publishing.

Song invited members to contribute best practices, cross-references, recommendations, and related materials, including references to OpenID Foundation work, OAuth, OIDC, and other identity-related efforts.

Chris asked whether the group is aiming for base recommendations, illustrative use cases, best practices, or something that could be measured for compliance. He noted that broad advice such as "do good security" is not enough; recommendations need enough precision that implementers can determine whether they align with the CG's guidance.

Song said he could draft an outline or framework for the protocol document and share it with the mailing list so that members can identify useful contribution areas.

### IX. Knowledge Mobility and Coordination with WebML

Chris suggested that the CG could take outputs already collected in this group and offer them to the WebMCP / WebML community as a checklist or punch list. This would help WebMCP participants ensure that their work covers identity, trust, security, and interoperability topics already discussed in the AI Agent Protocol CG.

He described this as a "knowledge mobility" opportunity: the CG can move relevant knowledge into the WebML group, and also bring relevant findings from WebML back into this CG. Song agreed that coordination with the Web Machine Learning Community Group would be valuable.

### X. Outreach to Linux Foundation and Other Standards Communities

Victor emphasized that it is important to understand W3C's role in the broader agentic AI protocol ecosystem. He noted that if many production deployments run in container or Kubernetes environments, then the Linux Foundation and CNCF ecosystems may have significant influence over which protocols gain adoption. From his perspective, MCP, A2A, and projects such as Cisco's Agency are currently central in that ecosystem.

Victor suggested that W3C may need to proactively reach out to the Linux Foundation to understand how W3C can contribute to or coordinate with that ecosystem rather than working in isolation.

Roy noted that the AI Agent Protocol CG is currently the W3C Community Group most directly focused on agent-to-agent communication protocols. He suggested that the chairs or participants could send a formal email to request liaison or collaboration with related organizations. Roy also suggested that, for a more formal W3C-level path, the group could coordinate through the **W3C Web and AI Interest Group**, which is a member group and may be better positioned to send liaison requests to other standards-development organizations.

The group agreed that a practical initial step could be to start with email outreach, understand the Linux Foundation's feedback, and then follow up through more formal W3C channels if appropriate.
