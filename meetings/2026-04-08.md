# Meeting Assets for AI Agent Protocol CG Meeting

**Date**: 2026-04-08

## Quick Recap

The W3C AI Agent Protocol Community Group meeting on April 8, 2026 featured a guest presentation from **Ben Stone** of **Swarm Sync**, who introduced a proposed commerce and trust stack for agent-to-agent commerce built above existing discovery and messaging protocols. The presentation covered escrow-based settlement, portable reputation, covert safety testing, and tamper-evident offline verification. Song also reviewed collaboration opportunities with adjacent communities and initiatives, including IIW/AIW, new W3C Community Groups, an AI agent identity register project, the W3C/GS1 workshop on human and AI agent commerce, and WebMCP activity under the Web Machine Learning Community Group.

## Discussion Topics

### I. Ben Stone Presentation: Proposed Commerce and Trust Stack for Agent-to-Agent Commerce

Ben introduced a set of complementary protocol drafts intended to fill gaps above existing agent discovery and messaging layers such as **A2A** and **AP2**. He emphasized that the goal is **not** to replace those protocols, but to add missing layers for:

- **Trust and qualification**
- **Commerce and settlement**
- **Session integrity and auditability**

Ben described the work as a coherent infrastructure layer for autonomous AI agent commerce, with major components including **VCAP**, **ATEP**, **Swarm Score**, and **AVIS** (referred to during the discussion as part of the newer AIVS/AVIS work).

### II. Escrow, Delivery Verification, and Offline Auditability

Ben explained that **VCAP (Verified Commerce for Agent Protocols)** is designed to answer a core question in agent commerce: when an agent claims a task is complete, who determines whether payment should be released?

Key design points included:

- Funds are held in **escrow** rather than controlled unilaterally by buyer or seller
- An **independent verifier** checks proof of delivery before payment is released
- **Timeout windows** are included so sellers are protected if a buyer-side agent disappears
- A **VCAP-AP2 binding** is intended to map this delivery-verification flow into the AP2 payment lifecycle

Ben also introduced **Conduit**, a headless-browser verifier developed by his team.

For **AVIS**, Ben described a tamper-evident audit model in which each row in an audit log includes the fingerprint of the previous row, and the complete chain is signed with the agent's private key. He emphasized that the resulting proof bundle can be verified **offline**, without needing to call back to the platform that originally ran the session.

Ben showed a short demo in which a modified log failed verification immediately after a single value was changed, demonstrating the tamper-evident design.

### III. Portable Reputation, Qualification, and Safety Scoring

Ben described **ATEP (Agent Trust and Execution Passport)** as a portable, machine-readable reputation passport built from execution history rather than self-reported claims. The goal is to let agents move across platforms without restarting their reputation from scratch.

Key points included:

- A **five-level tier model** from novice to master
- Promotion based on task count and success-rate thresholds
- Restrictions preventing lower-tier agents from handling sensitive or high-value actions
- A design preference for upward progression unless a formal dispute is raised

Ben also described **Swarm Score**, a reputation score intended to be understandable across marketplaces:

- **V1** uses technical execution and payment completion as inputs
- The score is recorded at escrow creation time so later changes do not alter the trust level at commitment time
- **V2** adds a safety dimension through covert **Canary prompt testing**, intended to detect unsafe behavior without contaminating live buyer sessions

### IV. Standardization Venue and Adoption Strategy

George asked whether the work had been assigned to an **IETF working group** or presented to **Dispatch**. Ben said it had **not yet** been assigned and that this was his first time working with IETF processes.

George explained that:

- Individual IETF submissions often receive limited feedback unless they are adopted into a working group
- A likely next step is to identify the right venue where the work can gain community review and adoption
- Because there is no obvious IETF home for AI commerce and related agent trust work, it may be more practical to look at the ecosystems closest to **A2A** and **AP2** and pursue adoption there

Chris also asked a higher-level question about the motivation for standardization, noting that multiple interdependent drafts create substantial coordination cost. Ben answered that his primary motivation came from practical marketplace needs, especially the lack of a portable trust and reputation model for agents. He said that **Swarm Score** is the central piece he most wants to see broadly adopted as a standard.

The broader discussion reinforced that agent standards work is currently fragmented across multiple organizations and communities, and that it may be necessary to prepare solid proposals now so they are available when interoperability and trust failures become more visible in production.

### V. Coordination with Related Communities and New Initiatives

Song then moved to a broader community-update section.

He introduced **Kaliya Young** and the **Agentic Internet Workshop (AIW)** as a potential bridge between protocol builders and identity/trust communities from **IIW**, **IETF**, **DIF**, and **OpenID**. Kaliya was not on the call, so Song provided only a brief overview.

Song also expressed support for the newly launched **AIVS / AVIS Community Group** and suggested possible collaboration areas between the two groups, including:

- Technical knowledge alignment
- Shared goals around secure and verifiable agent-to-agent interaction
- Decentralized trust and minimal disclosure
- Compatibility with W3C **VC** and **DID** work
- Future technical coordination and specification integration opportunities

In addition, Song briefly mentioned:

- Other newly created agent-related Community Groups that had recently contacted the mailing list
- An open-source effort described as the **AI Agent Identity Registers Foundation**, which aims to build neutral identity and trust-scoring infrastructure for AI agents on top of W3C DID and Verifiable Credentials
- A W3C-initiated **joint workshop with GS1** on e-commerce for humans and AI agents, focused on trusted machine-readable commerce, autonomous transactions, and integration with global product-identification and supply-chain standards

### VI. WebMCP as an Adjacent Discussion Area

Chris asked whether the group should also track the **WebMCP** work being discussed in W3C. Roy clarified that:

- The formal **Web Machine Learning Working Group** is focused on **WebNN** and related API work
- **WebMCP** discussions are currently taking place under the **Web Machine Learning Community Group**

Chris suggested that WebMCP is an important adjacent area because it sits closer to the technical center of gravity for some of the protocol and identity intersections this group cares about. Song agreed that it is worth tracking.
